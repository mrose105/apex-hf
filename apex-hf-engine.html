Why this engine exists

"I thought alpha meant beating the S&P 500."

It doesn't. Not even close. Alpha is return that is orthogonal to all priced risk. Mathematically — if you regress your portfolio returns on the market excess return and your intercept is statistically indistinguishable from zero, you have generated zero alpha. You've just been riding beta. The market carried you.

That reframed the way I think about the quant portfolio management problem. Rather than "how do I outperform?" — it's "what risks am I actually exposed to, and are they the ones I want?"

Risk has three layers — idiosyncratic (firm-specific), sector, and market. You can diversify away the first two. The third is supposed to be irreducible, but diversification is a statistical argument. It relies on low correlation between assets, and during market distress — like the tariff shock earlier this year, or the wars in the Middle East — correlation structures collapse. Everything moves together. The one "free lunch" in economics disappears exactly when you're most hungry for it.

This is why the law of large numbers doesn't save you in finance — returns are non-stationary. Your statistics don't converge.

Then comes the really elegant part. You can see these risk layers using PCA — eigendecompose your return covariance matrix and the first principal component, where every stock loads with the same sign, is literally the market factor made visible. PC2 splits by sector. Everything beyond that is where idiosyncratic noise and genuine alpha live. (The PCA panel above runs this decomposition live on APEX's four strategy pillars.)

CAPM formalises this:

Rᵢ − Rf = α + β(Rm − Rf) + ε
α = return independent of the market — actual skill  ·  β = sensitivity to the market factor — how much the market carries you  ·  ε = noise  ·  Rf = risk-free rate

The honest conclusion: most "alpha" is just uncompensated beta risk that happened to pay off in a bull market. "Everyone is a genius in a bull market."

That's what this engine is built to test. The CAPM regression panel above runs OLS on every backtest — if α is statistically significant and β is near zero, the four strategy pillars are generating returns the market can't explain. If β dominates, I'm just leveraging the S&P and calling it skill. The PCA tells the same story from a different angle: if PC1 eats most of the variance, the strategies are secretly correlated and there's hidden beta. The goal is a high decorrelation score — most variance living in the residual space where real alpha exists.

These aren't just diagnostic panels — they feed back into the engine in real-time. Every tick, the portfolio construction loop runs a 60-day rolling CAPM regression. If β drifts above 0.3, it actively de-weights vol and statarb (the strategies most likely to carry hidden market exposure) and rotates into macro (which provides crisis alpha — structurally negative β in drawdowns). Simultaneously, a rolling PCA eigendecomposition monitors whether PC1 concentration exceeds the Marchenko-Pastur random threshold (~42% for our T/N ratio). If it does, correlation is spiking — the "free lunch" is vanishing — and the engine reduces total exposure before the quant quake propagates.
